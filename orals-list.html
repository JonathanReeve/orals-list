2.6.1 Response

Both Synge's The Playboy of the Western World and D.H. Lawrence's Lady Chatterly's Lover were highly controversial, Playboy causing riots when it was first produced, and Lady Chatterly becoming the subject of a famous obscenity trial. The traditional explanations for this controversy—/Playboy/'s unromantic portrayal of peasant life, and Lady Chatterly's explicit sex scenes and uses of "four-letter Anglo-Saxon words," are not entirely satisfactory. Could the glimpses of female desire in both of these works have contributed to the public backlash against them?

Mina Loy's poetry also saw criticism for her use of sexual motifs, and her portrayal of the female gaze (as in "Hot Cross Bums"). For Loy as in Lawrence, these motifs are inextricably intermingled with ecological ones, and in particular, images of springtime, along with their mythological associations in Greek myth, and traditional fairy lore of the British isles. There is a sinister undertone to this, however, because whatever pastoral images Lawrence and Loy present are tinted with Waste Land-like prophetic visions of death and destruction. In Connie's love-drunk pastoral reverie in the clearing, for instance, she muses, "Perhaps this was one of the unravished places. Unravished! The whole world was ravished. / Some things can't be ravished. You can't ravish a tin of sardines. And so many women are like that; and men. But the earth…!"

The contrast of the green pastoral clearing with that of the mass-produced, non-perishable tin of sardines, is the kind of contrast that stitches together Eliot's The Waste Land. Images of sex and springtime are commingled with visions of corpses and desolation. Loy's poems, as well, feature this contrast. In "O Hell," it is tightly knit: "To clear the drifts of spring / Of our forebear's excrements / and bury the subconscious archives / Under unaffected flowers." This contrast is manifested stylistically, as well: operatic Latinate registers interrupt, and are interrupted by, chatty, conversational Germanic transcriptions. T.S. Eliot, in an essay on Stravinski's Rite of Spring, celebrates its music as "modern" and likens its choreography to that of "primitive ceremony." In particular, he adds, "the Vegetation Rite upon which the ballet is founded remained, in spite of the music, a pageant of primitive culture. It was interesting to any one who had read The Golden Bough and similar works…" Eliot's contrast of "modern" with "primitive" shows that he situates the societies Frazer discusses—most of which are actually his contemporaries—in a mythic past, or at early stages of a cultural evolution. Yet the "primitive" in Eliot and in Frazer—is never fully relegated to a distant place or time, but is a kind of menacing undercurrent that never stops quietly threatening "civilization." As Frazer puts it: "it is not our business here to consider what bearing the permanent existence of such a solid layer of savagery beneath the surface of society, and unaffected by the superficial changes of religion and culture, has upon the future of humanity. … We seem to move on a thin crust which may at any moment be rent by the subterranean forces slumbering below." After reading Frazer, the apocalyptic visions of The Waste Land seem born of anxieties over the invasions of spatio-temporally distant savageries or anarchies, especially those colonized, and even more especially those of colonized ancestors, whose traditions, like those of the Christmas tree, are embedded in those of "civilization."

Yet Frazer does not directly treat Christianity. In fact, Christianity is conspicuously absent. One would expect his chapter "Relics of Tree-worship in Modern Europe," for instance, which describes folk customs of may-poles and related traditions, to include at least a mention of the trees in his own culture that are ornamented and lit every year for Christmas. I suspect that his avoidance of Christianity reflects a kind of "closeted" atheism—"closet" in the sense of Sedgwick's Epistemology of the Closet, and "atheism" in a loose sense, meaning any outsider's understanding of the religion, especially an understanding of its myths as myths, and not necessarily truths. Given that Frazer's friend and colleague, the Old Testament scholar Robertson Smith was put on trial for heresy, and lost his job at Cambridge over a largely factual Encyclopaedia Brittanica article on the Bible, it is no surprise that Frazer's comparisons with Christianity have to be deeply implicit, if present at all.

This aspect of Frazer's rhetorical style—explicit and implied comparative anthropology—is likely just that which makes him appealing to modernist poets, since it is the logic of simile and metaphor. Take, for example, one of Frazer's ethnographic anecdotes illustrating his conception of "homeopathic magic": "if you are troubled with pimples, to watch for a falling star, and then instantly, while the star is still shooting from the sky, to wipe the pimples with a cloth or anything that comes to hand. Just as the star falls from the sky, so the pimples will fall from your body" (56-7). The logic of this practice is the logic of poetic simile.

Another factor which might help to explain why Frazer was so popular with the likes of Eliot, is that, as an archetypal "armchair anthropologist," Frazer is as much of a literary scholar as he is an ethnographer. He compares the "ancient books of the Hindoos" with Keats, and quotes Dickens characters to illustrate the workings of certain beliefs. Freud, too, in Civilization and Its Discontents, uses literary models to explain his psychoanalytic theories. Freud's etiology of civilized discontent—the paradox of unhappiness in a advanced society with so much infrastructure built to reduce unhappiness—is simply (and unsatisfactorily) that it is innate quality of human beings. Like the Greek god Thanatos, god of death, Freud's name for this "death drive," it is immortal. Freud's theory of this drive involves a phylo-ontogenic projection of his individual psychological theories, like the Oedipus complex, onto societies. Society-level phenomena, then, like religions, Freud explains in terms of tensions between ego and superego. Whereas the "cultural superego" is ethics, he argues, tensions between ethics and the cultural ego or id produce guilt and the "cultural neurosis" that he terms religions.

Freud, maybe because he is writing in 1929, or maybe because he is writing from the more religiously liberal Continent, does not display the same closeted atheism as Frazer. What is collected and removed (if somewhat spurious) in Freud seems a source of anxiety for the English Frazer and Eliot: the undercurrents of savagery that threaten to pull apart the fabric of civilization. Can we understand The Waste Land and analogous works of the same period as expressions of anxiety over the fragility of civilization?

  1. Why Study Literary Modernism with Computers?

    Most who work in the digital humanities sub-field of computational literary analysis (also termed "digital literary studies") study 19th Century texts, and with good reason. The major digital archives of public domain English-language literature—Project Gutenberg, the Oxford Text archive, and the British Library, to name but a few—have more texts that were published in the 19th Century than any other century. Since all 19th Century texts are in the public domain, this facilitates digitization and dissemination of the texts. The number of lesser-known texts in these corpora is tremendous, as has often been noted. This has variously been called "the great unread" or the "long tail" of literary archives, and presents many opportunities for the discovery of texts, using computational methods, that are of interest, despite being forgotten by the canon. Yet the early 20th Century—and the period of literary modernism, in particular, is an equally fertile field for quantitative literary research, and deserves more attention than is currently devoted to it.

    First, the early 20th Century is the frontier of the public domain, following the copyright laws of most English-speaking countries. Texts originally published in this era enter the public domain daily, and their digital editions may be freely shared online, and analyzed by computational text analysts. While this is still technically possible with contemporary, in-copyright books, amassing a large corpus of plain-text editions is both technically difficult and prohibitively expensive. In some cases, the digitization efforts of nations with more lenient copyright laws become useful for obtaining digital texts of this era—a text that enters the public domain in the United States might already be digitized by, say, Project Gutenberg Australia.

    Some text archives are specific to modernism. The ModNets network of archives aggregates metadata from about 70 peer-reviewed archives, ranging from small single-author archives, like Woolf Online and the Samuel Beckett Digital Manuscript Project, to larger archives, like the Modernist Journals Project. The Modernist Journals Project is a large corpus of literary magazines in which many now-canonical works of modernism were originally published. Many of the texts in the archive have been richly encoded in semantic markup like TEI XML (Text Encoding Initiative eXtensible Markup Language), enabling easy machine-readability. This corpus is so well-suited to computational analysis that the project has its own in-house analytic organization: the MJP Lab. ModNets also features works of lesser-known and popular literature. The Pulp Magazines Project, while not limited to works of the early 20th Century, provides an interesting counterpoint to the more well-known works of high literary modernism. Many works in this archive are also encoded in TEI XML.

    The availability of texts is not the only reason the 20th Century deserves more attention from computational text analysts, however. The stylistic experiments for which high modernism is famous are unusually well-suited to stylometry, or statistical approaches to literary style. Although there are not as many test cases for the classical uses of stylometric techniques, such as authorship attribution, as in periods with more anonymous texts, these techniques may be still used for literary critical purposes. Since many of the chapters of Ulysses are written in very different styles, for instance, we can treat the chapters as novels, and use statistical techniques to find stylistic affinities and distinctions among them, and among external texts. Similarly, a stylistic analysis of character speech in Ulysses shows which of James Joyce's characters speak the most alike, regardless of the content of their speech. The stylistic variation among these categories makes this novel a more fruitful site for this kind of analysis than some of the more narratively univocal Victorian novels.

    Other modernist works simply appear ready-made for computational analysis, due to their structural patterns. Virginia Woolf's experimental novel The Waves, for one, is written almost entirely as dialogue, in a form so regular—all the verbs are "said"—that it can be captured using a simple regular expression. This greatly facilitates its machine-readability, and allows for the automatic extraction of character speech. It is for this reason that the novel has been the subject of a few analyses, most notably Steven Ramsay's "Algorithmic Criticism", which employs an information extraction algorithm—TF-IDF—to find patterns distinctive of the novel's characters.

    The heightened intertextualities of modernist works also makes them unusually well-suited to computational comparisons with the works they cite. Using stylometric techniques, we might be able to quantify the stylistic similarities that have often been noted between Mrs Dalloway and Ulysses. Semantic analyses, too, such as those possible with word embedding algorithms like word2vec, allow for testing the content similarities between texts, and would be suitable for investigating affinities between Ulysses and its structural model, The Odyssey, or even among intergeneric pairs like Eliot's The Waste Land and the anthropological work that most influenced it, Frazer's The Golden Bough. While 19th Century literary texts are certainly not without intertextualities, modernist literature is famous for strong structural correspondences such as these, and its writers have widely theorized them—see Eliot's "Tradition and the Individual Talent," and "Ulysses, Order, and Myth."

    These kinds of comparisons may also be made between texts in a large corpus, in order to interrogate literary history. While the Victorian era, in British literature at least, is politically stable in comparison, the period of the early 20th century is full of political and social changes that are reflected in its literature. Quantitative analyses of literature of this period might help us to answer questions like: what effect did the first world war have on the rhythms of poetry written at this time? Can we see the women's suffrage movement reflected, in some way, in the word frequencies of novels of this period? Is the sexual liberation of the post-Victorian period detectable in any way in its prose? Finally, we might ask whether can we test, at least quasi-empirically, Virginia Woolf's claim that "on or about December 1910, human character changed."

    Of course, one potential side effect of this literary-historical quantitative analysis might be to problematize the very distinction between modernist and non-modernist literature. By studying the distinctive language patterns of Victorian and post-Victorian literature, or modernist and non-modernist literature, we might at least develop new vocabularies with which to describe the distinctions between these categories, if we don't acquire new knowledge about them. We might find that they are more distinct, by certain measurements, at least, than we previously believed, or, perhaps more likely, that the categories are more blurred than we imagined.

In The Waves, Virginia Woolf has her characters speak in utterances that only appear as dialogue by their juxtaposition with other utterances. Instead, they seem more like soliloquies: speeches to an audience that is not their fellow characters. As such, they possess certain affordances: they afford the intimacy of the dramatic soliloquy as confession, explanation, or Greek chorus. They also allow the story to congeal simultaneously from several independent points of view. As in an epistolary novel, the plot of the book (what little there is of it) comes into focus only through the layering of multiple perspectives. In some cases, it takes the accounts of all six major characters to get a complete picture of a single event. Since the narrative voice is minimal, and, as nature imagery, only relevant to the story by analogy, this constitutes a radical decentering of the narrative voice.
